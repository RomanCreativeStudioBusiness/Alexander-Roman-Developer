Overview

The site that sells the work I do

Roman Creative Studio is my own digital agency's marketing site, built to help local businesses — dentists, churches, home service companies, and other service-based businesses — find, trust, and book a strategy call with the agency. Because this site is the first proof point of what the agency can build for a client, it had to demonstrate results, not just describe services.

The site is organized around three core service pillars — Build (web design and development), Grow (SEO and local visibility), and Scale (digital strategy and automation) — with dedicated pages for high-value niches, a portfolio, transparent pricing, and a documented process.

Problem

Generic agency sites don't convert

Local business owners land on agency websites constantly, and most of them look identical: a portfolio grid, vague copy about "digital marketing," and a contact form that disappears into a void. Nothing tells the visitor why this agency is different or whether it can produce results for a business like theirs — so they leave without booking anything.

  • No clear next step for a first-time visitor to take.
  • Generic messaging that doesn't speak to a specific industry (dental practice vs. church vs. restaurant).
  • Claims without evidence — no real numbers to back up "we get results."
Goals

What the site needed to accomplish

  • Increase qualified inquiries — booked strategy calls — from local business owners, not just traffic.
  • Establish credibility for a new agency competing against firms with longer track records.
  • Support new niche landing pages (new industries) without a redesign each time.
  • Stay fast despite image-heavy portfolio and testimonial sections.
Research

What actually makes an owner trust an agency

Before writing a line of copy, I audited a dozen-plus competitor agency sites — local and national — and found the same pattern repeated: portfolio grid first, vague "we do digital marketing" messaging, and a contact form with no clear reason to fill it out. I also had informal conversations with the kind of business owners this site targets — a dentist, a church administrator — about what made them trust or distrust an agency's website before ever getting on a call. Specificity and proof came up every time; adjectives didn't.

Planning

Mapping the funnel before the layout

I planned the page as a single decision funnel before touching visual design: hero → proof (real case studies and testimonials) → services → process and pricing → one recurring call-to-action. I also decided upfront that niche landing pages (dental, church, and future verticals) needed to be a distinct content type from day one, with shared header, footer, and CTA components, so adding a new niche later wouldn't mean rebuilding shared structure from scratch.

Design Decisions

Choices that reinforce the one goal

  • Reserved a single accent color exclusively for CTA buttons, so "Book a free strategy call" never competes visually with anything else on the page.
  • Gave real numbers (47% increase, 3× growth) more visual weight than icons or badges — specificity was the strongest asset available, so the design foregrounds it.
  • Used generous spacing and restrained typography to read as premium rather than the cluttered, directory-style layout common among competitors.
Solution

Design around one decision: book the call

I built every page around a single primary call-to-action — booking a free strategy call — and structured the surrounding content to earn that click. That meant writing dedicated landing pages for specific niches (dental practices, churches) instead of one generic services page, so a dentist or church administrator sees language and proof built specifically for them.

I backed the pitch with real case studies and numbers instead of marketing platitudes: a 47% increase in new patient inquiries in 60 days for one dental client, and a 3× increase in first-time visitor registrations for a church client. Testimonials, a documented process section, and transparent pricing reinforce credibility the rest of the way down the page.

"Design decisions on this site are business decisions — a nicer layout only matters if it moves someone toward the call."

Technologies Used

Built for speed and easy iteration

The site is hand-coded with a custom asset pipeline rather than a page builder, which keeps it fast and easy for me to update as copy and offers change.

Architecture

How the pieces fit together

The site is a static multi-page build with shared component patterns rather than a single-page app: header, footer, and CTA blocks stay consistent across every page, including the growing set of niche landing pages, which keeps them from drifting out of sync as the site expands. Interaction logic lives in assets/js/main.js, separate from content in assets/images, so behavior and content can change independently. Cloudflare sits in front of the site for CDN caching and handles obfuscating the contact email against scraping.

Challenges

What was hard about this build

  • Credible proof, not fluff: Writing case studies that felt specific and honest instead of generic marketing claims — solved by leading with real client numbers instead of adjectives.
  • Scaling niche pages without duplicating the codebase: Built shared components (nav, footer, CTA blocks) so new niche landing pages could swap in industry-specific copy without rebuilding shared structure from scratch.
  • Staying fast with image-heavy sections: Portfolio and testimonial sections are naturally image-heavy — addressed with image optimization so load times didn't suffer as the site grew.
Trade-offs

Decisions made with eyes open

  • Hand-coded over a CMS: Chose static HTML/CSS/JS over WordPress or a page builder — faster and lighter, at the cost of no non-technical editing UI. Acceptable since I'm both the developer and the person updating copy.
  • Static niche pages over a templating system: Faster to ship initially, at the cost of some duplication across pages. A known trade-off I'd revisit with a static site generator if the number of niches kept growing.
  • No automated testing: Prioritized launch speed for a small business over building CI/test infrastructure, appropriate for a marketing site with no complex application logic.
Accessibility Considerations

Usable beyond a mouse and a monitor

  • Semantic landmarks (nav, header, sectioned main content) so screen readers can navigate by structure, not guesswork.
  • Checked color contrast on CTA buttons and body text against the dark backgrounds used across the site.
  • Kept visible focus states on links, buttons, and form fields instead of removing them for aesthetics.
  • Wrote descriptive link and button text ("Book a free strategy call" rather than "Click here").
Responsive Design Approach

Mobile first, because most visitors are

I designed single-column, stacked layouts first, then expanded to multi-column grids at wider breakpoints — the reverse of designing desktop-first and shrinking down. I tested manually at common breakpoints (375px, 768px, 1024px, 1440px), since most traffic for these clients arrives on mobile.

Performance Considerations

Fast despite heavy visual proof

  • Compressed and appropriately sized images across the portfolio and testimonial sections.
  • Kept JavaScript minimal — no framework overhead, since the site doesn't need client-side routing or complex state.
  • Relies on Cloudflare's CDN and caching for fast delivery regardless of visitor location.
Testing Process

How I verified it before and after launch

Testing was manual: cross-browser checks in Chrome, Safari, and Firefox across desktop and mobile, plus a keyboard-navigation and contrast pass for accessibility. There's no automated test suite — a reasonable trade-off for a marketing site with no complex application logic, but a gap I'd close with Lighthouse CI on a larger or more interactive project.

What I Learned

Front-end code serves the business goal first

This project taught me to treat front-end decisions as business decisions — every layout and copy choice gets measured against whether it moves a visitor toward booking a call. Because I keep iterating on this site post-launch based on real conversion data, it also pushed me to write clean, easy-to-edit markup instead of a one-time throwaway build, since I'm the one who has to change it every time the offer or messaging shifts.

Future Improvements

What I'd build next

  • Add automated Lighthouse checks on deploy to catch performance or accessibility regressions before they ship.
  • Migrate niche landing pages to a lightweight static site generator to remove copy-paste duplication between pages.
  • Add analytics-driven A/B testing on CTA copy and placement to keep improving conversion rate with data instead of intuition.
