Overview

Giving a weekly show a real home on the web

4Kingdom Podcast is a weekly Christian podcast I co-host with Micah, built around real conversations, biblical teaching, and encouragement for everyday life. I built the website to give the show an identity that exists beyond any single listening platform — a home base for the brand itself.

Problem

The show's presence was scattered

Before the site existed, the podcast lived across Spotify, YouTube, and social media with no consistent branding and no single link to send a new listener to. A new listener had no obvious "start here" experience, and the show had no home that actually reflected its identity and message.

  • No unified destination — links pointed to different platforms with inconsistent presentation.
  • No consistent visual brand across the platforms the show appeared on.
  • New listeners had no fast way to understand what the show was about before committing to an episode.
Solution

One hub, consistent brand, fast access to episodes

I built a single-destination site with a consistent brand system — name, tagline ("Real Conversations. Biblical Truth. Christ-Centered Growth."), color palette, and typography — applied across every page. The homepage explains what the show is and who it's for within seconds, and an episode section lets visitors browse and jump straight into an episode on Spotify or YouTube without leaving a confusing trail of links.

I treated the website as the hub and the listening platforms as distribution — not the other way around — so the brand identity stays consistent no matter where someone first discovers the show.

"The site had to work for an audience, not a customer — the goal is trust and repeat visits, not a single conversion."

Technologies Used

Modern deployment for a growing show

Built with a modern JavaScript front-end and deployed on Vercel, giving the site fast global delivery and reliable uptime as the show's audience grows.

Challenges

What was hard about this build

  • Warm, not generic: A podcast about real conversations needed a brand that felt personal rather than templated — solved with a deliberate, consistent visual language instead of a stock layout.
  • Scaling episode content weekly: The show adds a new episode every week, so the episode section had to stay easy to browse without needing a redesign every few months — solved with a structured, chronological layout built to grow.
  • Multi-platform without fragmentation: Linking out to Spotify and YouTube risked pulling the brand experience apart — solved by keeping the website as the consistent hub and treating outside platforms purely as distribution.
What I Learned

Designing for an audience, not a customer

This was my first time building a front-end for an audience rather than a client's customer — the incentives are different: engagement and trust over a single conversion event. It pushed me to think about content structure and repeat-visit experience instead of a single call-to-action. It also gave me hands-on experience with a second deployment model — Vercel — alongside Cloudflare on the Roman Creative Studio project, so I understand how front-end code ships differently depending on the platform.
