Overview

Giving a weekly show a real home on the web

4Kingdom Podcast is a weekly Christian podcast I co-host with Micah, built around real conversations, biblical teaching, and encouragement for everyday life. I built the website to give the show an identity that exists beyond any single listening platform — a home base for the brand itself.

Problem

The show's presence was scattered

Before the site existed, the podcast lived across Spotify, YouTube, and social media with no consistent branding and no single link to send a new listener to. A new listener had no obvious "start here" experience, and the show had no home that actually reflected its identity and message.

  • No unified destination — links pointed to different platforms with inconsistent presentation.
  • No consistent visual brand across the platforms the show appeared on.
  • New listeners had no fast way to understand what the show was about before committing to an episode.
Goals

What the site needed to accomplish

  • Give the show one consistent home across every platform it appears on.
  • Let a new visitor understand what the show is and start an episode within seconds.
  • Support weekly new episodes without needing a redesign as the archive grows.
Research

Learning from other independent shows

I looked at how other independent podcasts present themselves online and found most either had no dedicated site at all or an outdated one that didn't reflect the show's current identity. I also looked at brand-forward interview and media sites for patterns in how they organize episode browsing, and talked directly with Micah, my co-host, about tone and identity to make sure the design actually matched how the show sounds, not a generic "podcast template" look.

Planning

Three destinations, one identity

I planned the site around three core destinations: home (identity plus the latest episode), episodes (a browsable archive), and about (the hosts and the show's mission). I decided early that episodes should link out to Spotify and YouTube directly rather than embedding custom players — keeping the site simple, fast, and free of duplicating infrastructure those platforms already provide.

Design Decisions

Warm and consistent, not templated

  • Built one consistent type and color system reflecting the show's actual tone rather than a generic "corporate podcast" template.
  • Put the tagline — "Real Conversations. Biblical Truth. Christ-Centered Growth." — front and center so a visitor understands the show's identity in one glance.
  • Designed episode cards as a single reusable component so the browsing experience stays consistent as the archive grows.
Solution

One hub, consistent brand, fast access to episodes

I built a single-destination site with a consistent brand system — name, tagline ("Real Conversations. Biblical Truth. Christ-Centered Growth."), color palette, and typography — applied across every page. The homepage explains what the show is and who it's for within seconds, and an episode section lets visitors browse and jump straight into an episode on Spotify or YouTube without leaving a confusing trail of links.

I treated the website as the hub and the listening platforms as distribution — not the other way around — so the brand identity stays consistent no matter where someone first discovers the show.

"The site had to work for an audience, not a customer — the goal is trust and repeat visits, not a single conversion."

Technologies Used

Modern deployment for a growing show

Built with a modern JavaScript front-end and deployed on Vercel, giving the site fast global delivery and reliable uptime as the show's audience grows.

Architecture

How the pieces fit together

The site is built with a modern JavaScript front-end and deployed on Vercel, which gives room to add features later — like an automated episode feed — without re-architecting the site. The episode card is a single reusable component rendered for every entry, so the browsing experience stays visually consistent as new episodes are added each week, and outbound links to Spotify and YouTube are handled the same way across every card rather than one-off per episode.

Challenges

What was hard about this build

  • Warm, not generic: A podcast about real conversations needed a brand that felt personal rather than templated — solved with a deliberate, consistent visual language instead of a stock layout.
  • Scaling episode content weekly: The show adds a new episode every week, so the episode section had to stay easy to browse without needing a redesign every few months — solved with a structured, chronological layout built to grow.
  • Multi-platform without fragmentation: Linking out to Spotify and YouTube risked pulling the brand experience apart — solved by keeping the website as the consistent hub and treating outside platforms purely as distribution.
Trade-offs

Decisions made with eyes open

  • Link out instead of embedding players: Faster to ship and avoids duplicating hosting infrastructure Spotify and YouTube already provide, at the cost of visitors briefly leaving the site to listen.
  • Simple chronological list over search/filtering: Appropriate for the show's current episode count; I'd revisit this if the archive grows significantly.
  • No self-hosted media: Keeps the site lightweight, at the cost of not controlling the on-site listening experience directly.
Accessibility Considerations

Usable beyond a mouse and a monitor

  • Sufficient contrast between text and background across the brand color palette for readability.
  • Descriptive labels on episode links (platform and episode title, not just "listen here").
  • Logical heading structure so screen reader users can jump straight to the episodes section.
Responsive Design Approach

Mobile first, since audiences discover shows there

Podcast audiences overwhelmingly discover new shows on their phones, so I designed mobile-first: episode cards stack in a single column on small screens and move into a grid at wider breakpoints, with the same component styling at every size.

Performance Considerations

Light by design

  • Deployed on Vercel for fast global edge delivery.
  • Kept media playback off-site (Spotify and YouTube links) instead of self-hosting audio or video, avoiding heavy media payloads on the site itself.
Testing Process

How I verified it before and after launch

Testing was manual: cross-browser and cross-device checks, plus confirming outbound platform links (Spotify, YouTube) resolve correctly after every content update. There's no automated test suite — reasonable for a content site without complex application logic, but something I'd add if the site grew more interactive features like search or an embedded player.

What I Learned

Designing for an audience, not a customer

This was my first time building a front-end for an audience rather than a client's customer — the incentives are different: engagement and trust over a single conversion event. It pushed me to think about content structure and repeat-visit experience instead of a single call-to-action. It also gave me hands-on experience with a second deployment model — Vercel — alongside Cloudflare on the Roman Creative Studio project, so I understand how front-end code ships differently depending on the platform.

Future Improvements

What I'd build next

  • Add an RSS/episode feed integration so new episodes appear automatically instead of a manual update per release.
  • Add search and filtering once the episode archive grows large enough to need it.
  • Consider a lightweight embedded player for on-site listening, without giving up the simplicity of linking out today.
