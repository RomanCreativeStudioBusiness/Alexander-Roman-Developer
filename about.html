About me

I learned front-end development by shipping real websites for real businesses.

No bootcamp certificate, no CS degree — just projects that had to work, for clients who were paying attention to results, not effort.

My path into development

I started building websites out of necessity, not curiosity for its own sake. I run Roman Creative Studio, a small digital agency that helps local businesses — dentists, churches, home service companies — get a web presence that actually brings in customers. To deliver that, I had to become the developer, not just hire one.

That meant learning HTML and CSS properly, then JavaScript, then the parts of web development that never show up in a tutorial: what makes a client's phone start ringing, why a "beautiful" design can still convert at zero percent, and how to structure a page so a nervous first-time visitor knows exactly what to do next.

Alongside the agency, I built and launched 4Kingdom Podcast, a weekly show I co-host — which meant designing and coding a content platform from the audience's side instead of the client's: episode discovery, cross-platform links, and a brand identity that had to feel consistent everywhere it showed up.

Both projects taught me the same lesson from different angles: good front-end work is invisible when it's right and very visible when it's wrong. I'm now looking to bring that instinct into a team environment, where I can learn modern frameworks, testing practices, and component architecture from developers more experienced than me.

How I work

Principles I hold to on every build

Design for the visitor's decision

Every page has a job — book a call, subscribe, read the next episode. I design the layout around that one decision before I touch styling.

Performance is a feature

A slow site loses visitors before they ever see the design. I keep pages lean: optimized assets, minimal dependencies, fast first paint.

Responsive isn't optional

Most traffic for the clients I've worked with is mobile-first. I build and test mobile layouts first, then scale up.
